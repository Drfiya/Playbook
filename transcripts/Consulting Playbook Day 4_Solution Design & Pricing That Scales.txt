Part 1 - Current State Mapping (The Murder Mystery Framework)
Think of this like a murder mystery dinner: Someone murdered time. Someone murdered efficiency. Your job: Find out who, what, when, and why.
Worksheet 1 - Map All Current Tools:
Columns you need:
- Tool/Platform Name
- Type of Tool (AI or non-AI)
- Use Case (why they're using it)
- Department
- Number of Users
Why number of users matters: "If we do this first, it will affect 500 users. That's a third of your company." â†’ Gives you ammo for prioritization.
Worksheet 2 - Find Knowledge Sources:
Map every single knowledge source:
- Notion, Slack docs, Confluence, Jira, internal wikis
- Type of document
- Connected AI tools (including quick and dirty RAG setups)
- Where it's stored
- How often it's updated (critical for RAG viability)
- Size and volume
The RAG decision questions (always ask these):
"What data types? PDF, doc, image, video?"
"How big? How many?"
This tells you: Can we dump this in a large context window, or do you need proper RAG? If RAG, what level? Quick and dirty? Hybrid RAG? Agentic RAG (10,000 pages â†’ 100 page cheat sheet)?
Worksheet 3 - Document Processes:
Spend 1-2 hours mapping:
- Decision points
- Operational procedures
- Tacit knowledge they forget to mention
Warning: Even if they think they understand their own process, once you get into solutioning, tacit knowledge gaps will appear. This worksheet gives you a starting point.
Worksheet 4 - Identify the Owners (The Suspects):
Map who owns what:
- Business owners of each tool/process
- Technical owners (many companies have zero)
- Who holds the money (CFO? Controller? C-suite?)
Why this matters:
- Solutioning requires knowing the budget
- Understanding political allegiances ("Do you like Gamma because you've used it for 2 years, even though Claude 4.5 Sonnet now does the same thing?")
- Knowing who to contact for specific decisions
Part 2 - Baseline Assessment (Prove Your Value)
"Otherwise people will say we spoke to an AI consultant and accomplished nothing because you couldn't make it tangible."
Metrics that matter:
Before/After measurements:
- Time spent on manual tasks
- Error rates on processes (50 out of 100 applications are edge cases)
- Cost per transaction/output
- Employee satisfaction score
- Customer satisfaction metrics (if client-facing)
Target outcomes:
- % reduction in manual processing time
- Accuracy improvement targets
- ROI and cost saving goals
- User adoption rate targets
- Output quality improvements
The KPI framework structure:
For each metric, document:
- KPI name
- Baseline value
- Target value
- Measurement method
- Review frequency
Review frequency nuance: Don't measure day-by-day. Fridays = less productive. Mondays = less productive. Humans are humans. Weekly or monthly is better.
"The numbers are here as anchors for you. When you're grilled by C-suite, this is what you hold onto."
Part 3 - Implementation Rollout Strategy
Phase 1 - PILOT (30-60 days):
1. Select pilot group (count on one hand how many people)
2. Train core users
3. Run for 30-60 days
4. Collect feedback
5. Analyze results
6. Document wins in metric format (tangible reporting)
7. Document issues
8. Refine processes (assumed people always do X, Y, Z â†’ turns out only 60% of the time)
Adherence metric: "What % of people doing X continue doing X every Y frequency?"
Phase 2 - SCALE:
1. Train next cohort (much bigger group)
2. Deploy to entire department
3. Monitor adoption and adherence
4. Track metrics from pilot issues
Phase 3 - OPTIMIZE (3-6-12 months):
Expand gradually across organization.
Reality check: Some departments don't need AI for a while. Some are so messy with technical debt it's not worth expanding until you have proven momentum.
Goals:
- Track KPIs
- Identify bottlenecks (they keep popping up)
- Stay on top of model changes (3.5 Turbo deprecated mid-2026)
- Critical gaps identified
- Recommended next steps
"As models change and deprecate, bottlenecks will continue. Your responsibility: Either YOU monitor this, or give the organization what they need to do it themselves."
Part 4 - Centralized Strategy Checklist
Before implementation, know:
- Where SOPs, customer data, company knowledge lives
- Tool structure (using MCP servers? Which ones and why?)
- If they use Zapier for 90% of things, does it make sense to say "switch to N8N" just because you prefer it?
- Assess connectors, custom APIs, etc.
Department priorities for pilots:
Revenue-adjacent = highest value. Even 1% incremental positive change = tangible benefit = wind under your sails.

3. Marketing use cases:

Part 5 - Engagement Options & Pricing Tiers
The community downsell hack:
Instead of losing clients after 1-2 paid calls, create a mini-community (5-10 people):
"You don't have to pay $150/$500/$1000 for another 2-hour call. Get fractionalized access to me. I'll answer everyone's questions once a week in this community."
Could be on School, Circle, wherever. Price: $10-20/month.
Goal: Your foot stays in the door with people who otherwise wouldn't book another call.
Tight Budget Tier:
- $10-20/month community
- Your foot in the door
- Even if starting out, fractionalized access beats nothing
Medium Budget Tier:
- $250-500 per call
- Prepare beforehand (don't get informed minutes before)
- Minute 1 after weather talk = straight to value
- Bundle: Pay per session + answer X emails associated with the call
- Most popular: 15-minute calls (15 min prep, decent ROI)
Real Budget Tier (Retainer):
- $2K-10K/month
- Weekly check-ins
- Ongoing support by email (use templates so they don't send Hobbit essays)
- Small builds: GPTs, projects, automations you can repurpose
- Team training, priority access
- Bundles increase value
Template for decision point emails:
"When you send me a question about a decision point, frame it like: Problem, What You've Tried, Question."
Makes it easier for you to answer without syncing every time.
Enterprise Tier:
- $15K+ project-based
- Full implementation
- Multi-department engagements
- Subcontract developers to extend LTV and average order value
- Ongoing change management
- Public sector = way more expensive (nice numbers, but red tape/bureaucracy/audits)
"Instead of the Big Four getting it, why don't you get it?"
How to frame options when budget is unknown:
"I have three ways we can work together:
1. Most affordable: $20/month community
2. Middle ground: 30-minute or 15-minute call
3. Premium: Package of 4 one-hour calls for $1200"
Let them choose. Don't push. Having tiers increases likelihood of working together.
The brutal truth about knowledge vs relationships:
"In the next 12-18 months, all knowledge for knowledge's sake will be worthless. Relationships will be paramount."
Don't nickel and dime, but balance it. Don't be taken advantage of where they pay way less than the value you give in time spent.
Real example: Got email from former client about decision point. Two-sentence answer, one minute of thinking â†’ Didn't charge. Pro relationship over nickel and diming.
Part 6 - Be The Calm In The Chaos
"When you converse with a client, be the person they come to for calmness, not agitation or friction."
Most people in this space = overwhelmed and lost (deservedly so).
Phrases to use:
- "Ignore the noise"
- "Start simple"
- "You're not behind"
- "We'll build this correctly together"
Position yourself as a PARTNER, not a dollar sign relationship.
Always have a next step before next session:
Examples:
- List all the tools you have
- Map all data locations
- Define three use cases
- Get budget approval (could take multiple sessions)
- Pick pilot department
- Assign a champion
The 60-Minute Call Structure:
0-5 min: Pain and goal
5-15 min: Real discovery
15-25 min: Diagnosing
25-35 min: Recommendation (walk through why it might not be realistic for their business)
35-40 min: Next steps
Call checklist:
- Start with pain/goal
- Ask scale questions
- Map their stack (mentally if you think in workflows)
- Find knowledge gaps
- Give HONEST advice
- Warn of pitfalls
- Show alternatives (different ways to solve same thing, which are quicker/cheaper)
- Offer pricing tiers
"The more reps you get in, the more readily available this information will be for you."
